Brutalism, long associated with the cold, impersonal institutional buildings of the 1960s and 70s, is experiencing a surprising comeback in contemporary residential architecture. But we're not talking about the same brutalism.

## From raw concrete to high-performance concrete

The new generation of architects adopts the visual language of brutalism — clear volumes, exposed concrete, honest geometry — and wraps it in modern insulation and energy efficiency technologies. Today's concrete is not the same as 50 years ago: advanced compositions allow for a reduced carbon footprint, and specialized formwork offers fine, almost tactile textures.

## Where we see this trend

1. **Urban villas** that contrast with the historical fabric through pure volumes and generous windows
2. **Vacation homes** that emerge from the ground like rock outcrops, integrating into the landscape
3. **Extensions and conversions** that deliberately contrast old (brick, wood) with new (concrete, glass)

## Why it works

The public has grown tired of the universality of drywall. Exposed concrete communicates solidity, permanence, material truth — qualities that a family home instinctively demands. And when combined with warm wood and carefully calibrated natural light, the result is not cold, but deeply serene.
